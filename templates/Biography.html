Biography
Winston Churchill once said: “Success consists of going from failure to failure without loss of enthusiasm.” Growth does not come from success, but rather, comes with learning from failures. The determination that humans have to persevere through failure is truly remarkable and I aspire to be one of those people. Born and raised in San Diego, I was always outside trying to absorb as much of the world (and the sun) as I could. I was a part of the Boy Scouts Of America during my youth, which allowed me to see more of the world than my backyard. I went on many excursions during my time in scouts, with the honorable mentions of: backpacking over 100 miles in New Mexico, eating fresh lobster while sailing in the Florida Keys, and viewing the starscape while trying to sleep in a cave in the Mojave Desert. I learned from these adventures how to interact with people with confidence and how to properly be a leader.
My academic growth started with a book. The book, “The Problem of Increasing Human Energy,” by Nikola Tesla, described how magnetic fields can be manipulated to create controllable arcs of electricity. My curiosity quickly grew into an obsession and, with my father’s help and a year’s worth of solder burns, I produced my first musical Tesla coil. The satisfaction of first flipping that switch meant more to me than scoring in rugby or receiving an A in a class. I knew I had to be an engineer and college was a necessity. I decided to move away from home to pursue a degree in chemical engineering at Oregon State University (OSU).
The summer after my sophomore year at OSU, I interned at Funai Corporation under Dr. Manish Giri. This project required the development of MATLAB code and the use of machine shops to help fabricate the microfluidic analytic system Helios®. The device was designed to print and read 1,000 cell cultures on a single 3 by 5 inch glass plate. My contribution enabled the device to photograph the printed cell cultures and analyze the samples for particulates. With my contribution, the endless hours of counting cultures under a microscope will now be reduced to several minutes. I loved the intensity of the work. I enjoyed struggling through hundreds of lines of code to create a final product that would ultimately benefit society. This project opened many doors to me by introducing a new aspect of chemical engineering that was not typically taught in school.
That summer, I also started my research with the Corona Project with Dr. Nick AuYeung. I created a series of alternating current (AC) circuits that boosted the input voltage of 120 volts to up to 12,000 volts with an adjustable frequency and duty cycle controller. The circuit was implemented into a micro-reactor to stimulate a corona discharge in a channel of flowing gas. This research project provided a mechanism for creating fuel additives by applying high voltages to short-chain hydrocarbons. The determination and critical thinking skills developed in these undergraduate projects will directly apply to my school work.
I expanded my research into a different field during my internship at Fraunhofer UMSICHT in Sulzbach-Rosenberg, Germany. I designed and conducted inductive hydrotreating experiments to understand effects of magnetic fields on crude bio-oil samples from a Thermo-Catalytic Reformer (TCR®). I also undertook a catalytic refining project to determine the most efficient reaction parameters to minimize nitrogen and oxygen concentrations in crude oil samples. I created a Design Of Experiments (DOE) to compare the effectiveness of CoMo/Al2O3 and NiMo/Al2O3 at different temperatures, pressures, and resonance times. These catalysts reduced oxygen and nitrogen counts of compounds in TCR® samples from 4.5 to 1.3 wt.% and from 7.0 to 0.8 wt.%, respectively. The difficulty and rigor of the project at Fraunhofer UMSICHT further inspired me to pursue a chemical engineering graduate degree. I saw how the research in reusable fuels directly contributed to benefiting the community by reducing and reusing resources in incredibly unique methods. My experience abroad in Germany opened my eyes to a world beyond Oregon State University. I learned to balance my work life and personal life as I traveled across the country for leisure and for business. Taking advantage of this travel experience, I established professional relationships with colleagues and professors. I was able to collaborate with others, in German and English, to complete objectives while meeting rigid deadlines.
Through these experiences, I learned that I wanted to play a role in the clean-energy industry. I decided to continue my education at the University of Washington as a graduate student under Dr. Eric Stuve. Bright-eyed and bushy-tailed, I quickly started on an electrochemistry project aimed at improving the lives of those suffering from renal failure. The project included frequent meetings with business sponsors to collaborate with other groups to develop a working prototype of a wearable artificial kidney (WAK). With my help, our group designed Ni(OH)2 catalysts for improved current density (from 2.5 to 10mA cm -2) for the removal of urea in dialysis fluid. Unfortunately, there is a sufficient amount of chloride ions in dialysate fluid, which actively corrode Ni(OH)2 catalysts. The information from Ni(OH)2 catalysts paved the way for the use of nickel-based alloys as an alternative. These materials show catalytic activity and are relatively passivated in brackish water. With our project cut short, I learned that fundamental research is important but can't always pay the bills to keep the lights on.
My next project was focused on the development of Ni(OH)2 catalysts to gain a complete understanding of the urea oxidation reaction. I designed and built a bi-functional high-vacuum system to melt 99.999% nickel wire with electron bombardment to synthesize nickel-single crystals. These crystals were taken and tested for their crystalline and urea oxidation properties. Coupled with this project, I built another vacuum system to measure the gaseous products to determine the urea electrooxidation reaction pathways. These two systems, combined, laid the groundwork for Ni(OH)2 catalysts and paved the way for future fundamental studies.
I commonly say "undergraduate is where you learn things, where graduate school is where you learn how to learn things." I learned electrochemistry with papers, conferences, and textbooks - as there was not an electrochemistry class I could take. I learned to break a complex problem into many solvable components. I believe that this method of thinking is applicable to all subjects and industries and excites me to perform at maximum effort to benefit myself, my peers, and my community.
