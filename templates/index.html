Renewable Energy: The Future
My research in graduate school was focused on the the conversion of urea, a small organic molecule, into usable forms of energy and fuel through electrochemical oxidation. Urea is a small organic molecule that is continually produced, circulated, and consumed through natural processes. Unfortunately, urea is a common pollutant in waste streams from industrial processes, human excrement, and drug production. High concentrations have been seen to disrupt the natural process of the nitrogen cycle, causing negative environmental impacts. The high concentrations of urea can be removed by several different types of processes, but electrochemical removal poses a double benefit. Electrochemically oxidizing urea allows for urea to removed from the environment with hydrogen as a byproduct.

Prior to graduate school, my research experience was converting organic matter into thermo-chemical energy storage mediums through thermocataltyic cracking of complex hydrocarbons (such as coffee grounds from the office) into usable blends of gasoline diesel. Additionally, the synthesis gas from this process was used as feedstock into a high-voltage microreactor to increase product yield.
